Pastry Knight

For 6 months I worked on the making of Pastry Knight, a rogue-like / dungeon crawler reminiscent adventure that follows the brave Pastry Knight on his tumultuous path to rescuing his daughter, as part of Game development minor. During this period I learnt the programming language C# and work using the game editor Unity.

In my team I was in charge of designing and coding the any UI / HUD the game had, but I was also involved in: the storytelling of the game, flow of the story, coding some of the game mechanics used in game.

The process

Once a team was established we all: brainstormed ideas for a story we would like to tell, made a collection of games genre we love, and a collection of games aesthetic we want our game to have.

Everyone added their ideas to digital board and a mood word was made for the visuals, but also clusters of story idea were made.

We all, in our free time, dote voted on the idea we liked the most and had a call to discuss the direction we wanted to go as a group for the concept art and visuals.

We ended up with Pastry Knight, a rogue-like / dungeon crawler set in a pastrified (turned into pastry) parallel world following a retired knight on his journey to save his daughter. The knight (player) must smash his way through pastry enemies and advance through the world.

UI

Main Menu

A list of content needed in the main menu was made by using other popular game's main menu content as inspiration. With the list done, I asked the concept artists to send me some of the concepts they had made so that i may use it in the menu page.

I then moved on to designing the first mockups of the main menu using Figma (version 1 & 2) but none of them were appealing to me or the team, so after more inspiration and a play test where I got see the other teams games; I made the final version which was very well received in the team.

Option Screen

The options screen started in the same way as the main menu, we looked around at games we are familiar with and noted down the most common settings. Once I had a good list of settings I sat down with our programming team and checked which settings were feasible to have in the game and which were not. With a list in hand we did card sorting to categories them and be able to screen options accordingly.

The first version of the options screen was done before the main menu was making use of the background to place buttons, therefore it was made in a way that does not match the final main menu. It made use of a tab system to separate the options.

To match the main menu, the tree from the background expands to reveal the opinions screen and its different options. Version 2 made use of a tab system but after more discussion with the programming team some of the sections became very empty.

In version 3 all the options where placed under one single tab and where sectioned off by headers itavoid having tabs with one single option in it.

Unfortunately due to time constraint we had to cut a lot of the options that were not yet implemented, leaving us with the final options screen iteration.
